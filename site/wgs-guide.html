Genomics primer

Whole genome sequencing, explained practically.

Whole genome sequencing reads across nearly all of your DNA instead of checking only a small set of predefined markers. That makes it flexible: the same data can be revisited as reference genomes, callers, annotations, and research improve.

WGS vs microarray vs exome

These tests answer different questions. WGS is broad, microarrays are marker-based, and exomes focus on protein-coding sequence.

Whole genome sequencing

Attempts to sequence the full genome: autosomes, sex chromosomes, mitochondrial DNA, coding regions, non-coding regions, and many structural signals.

broadest reuse

Microarray genotyping

Checks selected markers chosen by a chip design. It is common for ancestry, but misses most variants that are not on the chip.

cheapfixed markers

Exome sequencing

Targets protein-coding regions. Useful for many clinical questions, but it skips most non-coding and structural context.

coding focus

The usual file flow

Most WGS analysis moves through a small set of file types. WGS Extract helps at several points in this chain.

FASTQRaw reads and base quality scores directly from sequencing.
BAM / CRAMReads aligned to a reference genome, sorted and indexed for access.
VCF / BCFVariant calls: positions where the sample differs from the reference.
Reports / kitsMicroarray simulations, haplogroups, annotations, extracts, and filtered outputs.

Coverage and quality

Coverage is how many reads support a position. A "30x" genome means an average of about 30 reads cover each position, but real coverage varies across GC-rich regions, repeats, sex chromosomes, mitochondrial DNA, capture gaps, and low-mappability regions.

Coverage questions to ask

  • Is this average coverage or breadth of coverage?
  • Are mitochondrial and Y-chromosome reads present?
  • Was the sample aligned to hg19, hg38, T2T, or another build?
  • Is the alignment sorted and indexed?
  • Are low-quality reads or duplicate reads affecting calls?

Variant types

Different callers and workflows target different kinds of changes. No single command is best for every variant class and sequencing technology.

Reference builds and coordinates

A genomic coordinate only makes sense relative to a reference build. chr1:100000 in hg19 may not refer to the same biological position in hg38 or T2T.

hg19 / GRCh37

Older but still common in genealogy tools, legacy annotations, and many older consumer datasets.

hg38 / GRCh38

Modern mainstream reference for many current short-read WGS pipelines and annotation resources.

T2T

Telomere-to-telomere references cover many regions older references represented poorly, but tool and annotation support varies.

Do not mix builds casually. If your BAM was aligned to hg38, call variants and annotate with hg38-compatible resources unless you intentionally lift over coordinates or re-align.

What WGS can be used for

WGS is powerful, but it is not magic. Results depend on data quality, tool choice, reference build, ancestry representation in databases, and careful interpretation.

Ancestry and genealogy

Microarray simulations, Y/MT extracts, haplogroups, triangulation, and upload-compatible files for third-party tools.

Variant exploration

SNP, InDel, SV, CNV, VEP, ClinVar, gnomAD, conservation, splicing, and pharmacogenomic annotation workflows.

Data stewardship

Convert BAM to CRAM, index files, verify references, preserve metadata, and keep data private and backed up.

Privacy and medical caution: Whole genome files can reveal sensitive information about you and biological relatives. This site is educational and does not provide medical advice.
